DebateDash case study

From social idea to revenue-generating debate platform.

A real-time social debate platform that turns argument into a competitive, engaging product loop.

Overview

DebateDash combines posts, communities, real-time PvP debate arenas, AI practice opponents, and reputation into a single platform. I took it from concept to production over 8 months, serving 20k+ users and generating revenue through advertising.

Visit DebateDashRead the build-in-public post

The challenge

Debate is naturally adversarial. The product had to make argument feel social, fair, and repeatable—not toxic or one-sided.

  • Keep debates fact-based and non-biased without human moderation scaling costs.
  • Support real-time PvP matchmaking and social feeds in a small-team setting.
  • Ship an MVP fast while leaving room for growth, monetization, and AI features.

Constraints

  • Small team: founder + 2 developers + 1 QA engineer.
  • Hard deadline: MVP in 3 months, production launch in 8 months.
  • AI costs and latency had to be managed carefully from day one.
  • Reputation, moderation, and privacy requirements from launch.

Architecture

Next.js frontend, Supabase for auth/real-time database, LLM services for AI judge and practice opponents, Mixpanel for analytics, and dedicated email providers for notifications.

Major technical decisions

  • Schema first: defined users, friendships, participants, match states, posts, comments, notifications, and reputation before polishing the UI.
  • Skeleton before scale: shipped the core loop—create a post, create a debate, join it, save data—then expanded.
  • Custom core, bought telemetry: built debate mechanics in-house, used Mixpanel for product analytics.
  • Purposeful AI: used AI for practice opponents, argument generation, and judging—not as a superficial add-on.

Tradeoffs

  • Custom debate engine vs. off-the-shelf forum: chose custom to own the core product loop.
  • AI judge vs. human moderation: AI for speed and scale, with fact-based guardrails.
  • Build vs. buy analytics: bought Mixpanel to stay focused on the product.

Metrics

3 monthsMVP shipped.
8 monthsProduction launch.
20k+Users served.
RevenueGenerating through advertising.

Lessons

  • Partner early on requirements and timelines; unclear scope kills velocity.
  • Balance hands-on coding with leading the team—both are delivery risks if neglected.
  • AI-native workflows (Windsurf/Devin) accelerate iteration but still need strong judgment.
  • Schema-first design prevents the most expensive rewrites.
  • Good documentation is a force multiplier for QA and new developers.

GiveGigs case study

Your schema is the single source of truth for AI tooling.

A freelance marketplace moderation dashboard that surfaced why your schema must be a single source of truth.

Overview

GiveGigs is a freelance marketplace similar to Fiverr. I built a full role-based moderation system and dashboard using Next.js and Supabase. During development, an untracked manual schema change caused schema drift that broke local environments, type generation, CI pipelines, and AI-assisted coding tools.

Open GiveGigs

The challenge

Build a reliable role-based moderation system while keeping the database schema trustworthy across developers, tooling, and AI assistants.

  • Moderation views needed strict, role-based access controls.
  • AI coding assistants were generating code against tables and columns that only existed in production.
  • Onboarding new developers failed because their local schema didn't match production.
  • The root change was hidden—nobody had documented or admitted the manual edit.

Constraints

  • Small, distributed team with shared database access.
  • CI, type generation, and AI tools all depended on migration history matching reality.
  • Stopping to fix drift delayed feature work, but continuing would compound the damage.
  • No formal schema-change permissions or audit process existed yet.

Architecture

Next.js frontend, Supabase for auth/real-time database, Row Level Security policies, and role-based access for admins, moderators, and users.

Major technical decisions

  • Schema-first recovery: mapped the actual production schema against migration history before writing any fix.
  • Clean migration state: removed the incorrect migration entry rather than layering another band-aid.
  • Reapply and normalize: re-ran migrations until source control, migration history, and the database matched exactly.
  • Process over speed: introduced permission controls and review rules for all future schema changes.

Tradeoffs

  • Stop feature work to fix drift vs. ship around it: chose root-cause fix to prevent compounding errors in AI-generated code.
  • Strict migration process vs. one-off convenience: chose process to protect the team's velocity long-term.
  • Audit existing access vs. blame: focused on controls and documentation, not individuals.

Metrics

100%Schema alignment restored across local, staging, and production.
0AI-generated references to phantom schema objects after fix.
ReproducibleNew developers could spin up a matching database state.
ControlledSchema changes now require migration + review.

Lessons

  • Treat migration history as source code, not as a deployment side effect.
  • Never apply production schema changes without a migration.
  • Review migration files with the same rigor as application code.
  • Keep schema history under version control and standardize who can change what.
  • Regularly verify that local, staging, and production schemas match.
  • A single unrecorded change can silently break AI tooling, CI, and new-developer onboarding.

QualifyAI case study

Turn any prospect website into a sales-ready brief.

An AI-powered lead qualification platform that helps agencies identify the best inbound opportunities before sales outreach begins.

Overview

An agency pastes a company's website URL. QualifyAI analyzes the business, evaluates its marketing maturity, audits SEO, detects missing analytics pixels, reviews Google reputation signals, assigns a lead score, and drafts a personalized cold email.

Watch demo

The challenge

Inbound lead qualification is repetitive and inconsistent. Agencies need to research a prospect, identify gaps, decide whether the opportunity is worth pursuing, and personalize outreach—all before a salesperson starts a conversation.

  • Collect meaningful sales signals from a single website input.
  • Turn analysis into an understandable, actionable qualification score.
  • Give sales teams a relevant outreach starting point instead of a generic template.

Workflow

1. Paste websiteStart with the prospect's public website.
2. Analyze signalsEvaluate SEO, marketing maturity, tracking, and reviews.
3. Score the leadPrioritize prospects using automated qualification signals.
4. Prepare outreachGenerate a personalized sales brief and cold email draft.

Key product decisions

  • Website-first input: reduce friction by starting from information agencies already have.
  • Explainable qualification: surface the analysis behind the lead score so a rep can validate it.
  • Sales-ready output: connect intelligence directly to a personalized outreach draft.
  • Multiple signals: combine technical, marketing, and reputation signals instead of relying on one metric.

Product screens

Business impact

  • Helps agencies qualify inbound leads automatically before sales outreach.
  • Reduces repetitive prospect research and improves consistency across the pipeline.
  • Gives sales teams a faster path from a website URL to informed, personalized outreach.
